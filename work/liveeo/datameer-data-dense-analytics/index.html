CASE STUDY · DATAMEER · 2016 – 2021

How do you make big data feel simple?

Director of Design

A collaborative analytics platform. Top US, Canadian, and UK banks. Data so complex that the tools themselves created confusion.

§ 01

The starting point

When I joined Datameer in 2016, the platform had been built by engineers for engineers. It was powerful but impenetrable. Data analysts at top US, Canadian, and UK banks and insurers were using it daily — people who understood data deeply but needed the interface to get out of their way, not add cognitive load on top of already complex analytical work.

The design function existed but was purely execution-focused. Designers received specs and made them look good. There was no user research, no design strategy, no seat at the product table.

✱
The query builder showed empty results. The full data existed. Users thought the system was broken.

The sample-based uncertainty problem — the hardest UX challenge on the platform

§ 02

The core challenge

Big data analytics platforms have a fundamental UX problem: when you're working with datasets too large to process in real-time, you show samples. But samples can be empty — not because the data isn't there, but because the sample didn't capture it.

Users would build a query, see empty results in the preview, and assume their data was missing. They'd file support tickets, redo their work, lose trust in the tool. The actual data was fine. The preview was lying to them — not through a bug, but through a fundamental limitation of sampled data.

This wasn't a visual design problem. It was an information design problem — how do you honestly represent uncertainty without making users lose confidence in the tool they depend on daily?

§ 03

Approach

  1. 01

    Customer Research

    I secured exec-sponsored access to visit regulated enterprise environments — banks and insurers in the US — joining the sales team on customer site visits. This gave us direct insight into how analysts actually worked, not how we assumed they worked.

  2. 02

    Platform Redesign

    Complete overhaul of the analytics interface — from information architecture down to pixel-level craft. Column charts, big-data pivot tables, aggregated JSON structure views, result cards, and a comprehensive filter system.

  3. 03

    Design System

    Built the platform's design system from scratch — the first unified visual language for a product that had grown organically for years. Created shared components, patterns, and documentation.

  4. 04

    Organizational Elevation

    Moved design from execution-only to strategic partner — earned a seat at the product table by demonstrating the business value of user-centered design in a deeply technical product.

§ 06

What I learned

The hardest part wasn't the design — it was earning the organizational credibility to do user research in banks that treated every external access as a compliance risk. I had to prove that design could think strategically before anyone would let us talk to users. The redesign was the proof; the research access was the reward.

This experience shaped my conviction that you can't build good products without building the organization that supports them. Human-centered organizations make better products — because they actually talk to humans.

WHY THIS MATTERS FOR YOUR TEAM

If you're building data-dense tools for technically sophisticated users in regulated environments, the design challenges are fundamentally different from consumer products. I've spent five years learning those differences — from sample-uncertainty communication to compliance-aware user research. This is transferable to any domain where the data is complex, the users are expert, and the stakes are high.
