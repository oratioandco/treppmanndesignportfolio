CASE STUDY · NINOX SOFTWARE GMBH · 2023 – 2024

Leading a team is a design problem

Head of Product Design

At Ninox I was Head of Product Design: a team of five, a seat at the leadership table, and a design practice to turn into a real function. The part I think about most was none of that. It was a team that had quietly given up on one of its own.

§ 01

The role

I joined Ninox to make design a real function. The company was shifting from a low-code tool to an AI-powered no-code platform, and design needed to carry the same weight as product and engineering, not sit downstream of them.

Most of that was the expected work. I managed a team of five, including a design lead. I built a planning process so design, product, and engineering worked from the same picture. I set up shared governance of the design system across four designers and engineering. I hired people, and when someone was not the right fit, I made that call too.

The part that taught me the most was not on that list.

✱
When collaboration breaks down, the first question is 'what is wrong with our system?', not 'what is wrong with that person?'

The reframe at the centre of how I lead

§ 02

The engineer the team worked around

One engineer had been quietly labelled difficult. Meetings with him were tense. His points got cut off before he finished them, and the team had learned to route around him instead of working with him.

It did not add up. His code was excellent. His ideas, once I gave them room, were often the sharpest in the room. So I started watching the pattern instead of the person.

He struggled in fast, talk-over-each-other discussions, but he was precise in writing. He needed time to think before he answered. What the team read as interrupting was him returning to a point the meeting had already rushed past. This was not a diagnosis. It was a design constraint. Our collaboration ran on one default — fast, verbal, loudest-voice-first — and his mind did not fit it.

§ 03

What I changed

  1. 01

    Agendas, shared early

    Every meeting got a written agenda in advance. No more "quick syncs" that turned into hour-long debates no one could prepare for.

  2. 02

    Room to prepare

    Context went out before group discussions, so processing could happen ahead of the meeting rather than under time pressure inside it.

  3. 03

    Turn-taking, on purpose

    In group settings I made space for sequential input, instead of letting whoever spoke loudest set the outcome.

  4. 04

    Decisions in writing

    Important decisions were written down, not left to whoever remembered the conversation their way.

  5. 05

    Coaching the design lead

    I coached my design lead through the same relationship — how to work with a difficult engineering counterpart directly, rather than around them.

§ 04

The pushback

Not everyone agreed at first. "Why should we change how we work for one person?" — sometimes asked out loud, sometimes through eye-rolls and foot-dragging.

It became a conversation about equity versus equality. Equality is everyone getting the same thing. Equity is everyone getting what they need to contribute. Different minds need different conditions, and that is accommodation, not special treatment.

I also made the plain business case: our process was filtering out good ideas before they were heard. That is a cost, not just a fairness question.

§ 05

What changed

The "difficult" engineer
Tension, dismissed ideas, the team working around him→One of the strongest contributors — ideas got airtime, and he caught problems others missed
Team norms
Loudest-voice-first discussions→Written agendas, prep time, and documented decisions became how the team worked by default
How the team thought about friction
People started asking what was wrong with the process before deciding what was wrong with a person

Within months the dynamic shifted. The bigger change was that the structured habits spread. Other people adopted the prep, the agendas, the written decisions for themselves — they stopped being an accommodation and became the norm. The same attention I gave to one engineer's working conditions is the attention I give to product design: understand the real need, design for the edge case, check the assumption.

§ 06

What I took from it

Good leadership is not mainly about managing people. It is about designing the conditions people work within. When collaboration breaks down, look at the system before you look at the person.

And you cannot build inclusive software with an exclusive team process. Human-centred teams are what make human-centred products possible.
