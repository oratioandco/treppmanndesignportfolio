CASE STUDY · BIBEL TV · 2025 – PRESENT

A Copilot Skill for Customer Care

Head of Product Design

AI drafts a response to each incoming letter or email using the CS handbook as context. A support team member reads it, edits or rewrites, then sends. The blank-page work shrinks; the judgment stays with the team.

§ 01

What this is — and what it isn't

This is a Copilot skill, not a custom-built agent. The platform does the heavy lifting; the design work was scoping what the skill should do, loading the right context, defining what 'a good draft' looks like in Bibel TV's tone, and building the workflow around it.

It isn't a custom-trained model, it doesn't have a self-learning feedback loop, and it doesn't auto-send. The team sees the draft, decides, and sends. The skill just compresses the blank-page part of the job.

§ 02

The Problem

Bibel TV gets a steady stream of letters and emails from viewers — many of them sensitive, about faith, personal questions, or grief. Each reply has to feel pastoral and warm, follow the company's customer-support handbook, and address the specific concern. Drafting from a blank page eats hours per week, and tone consistency drifts depending on who's writing.

§ 03

The design decisions

Handbook is the context, not the model

The skill loads the CS handbook — tone guidelines, common questions, response templates, escalation rules — as context with each draft. The model isn't trained on it; it just reads it alongside the incoming message. Handbook updates take effect the moment the document changes. No retraining, no pipeline.

Define 'good draft' before tuning the prompt

Worked with the CS team to spell out what a good draft looks like for the most common message types: tone, length, structure, whether to reference a Bible verse, when to suggest a one-on-one call. That target gave the prompt-tuning something concrete to aim at instead of generic 'make it sound nice.'

Human keeps the send decision, always

The draft never goes out without a human edit-and-send. This isn't an efficiency tradeoff — it's the design point. For sensitive communications, the human judgment is the product. The skill moves the blank-page problem; nothing more.

Escalation as a first-class output

Some incoming messages shouldn't get a drafted response at all — acute crisis, edge cases, anything where a confident-sounding draft would be worse than no draft. The skill is configured to flag those rather than draft them, so the team gets a clean signal instead of a polished-but-wrong starting point to reject.

§ 04

What I took from it

A Copilot skill is a small piece of design surface but a useful one to do well. The leverage isn't in the model — it's in scoping what context to give it, what counts as a good output, and where the human stays in charge. Those are design decisions, not engineering ones.

The principle that ports to other domains: AI augments judgment, it doesn't replace it. In customer support, in legal drafting, in any domain where the human is liable for the output, the design job is to compress drafting time while leaving the judgment surface untouched.
