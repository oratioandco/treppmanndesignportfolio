## Quick Wins — Articles Tobias Can Write in a Day

### Quick Win 1: "I Don't Need Your Analytics"
**Estimated time:** 4-6 hours  
**Why it's quick:** Tobias already knows all the methods by heart. He's lived this constraint at 4 companies. The article structure writes itself:
- Hook: "I've spent the last decade designing products I can't put analytics on."
- Method 1: Guerilla usability testing with usertesting.com iterative screening (Datameer)
- Method 2: Stakeholder research as proxy for user data (Hypoport)
- Method 3: Direct customer conversations and feedback loops (ChurchDesk)
- Method 4: Qualitative validation through support ticket analysis
- Why this matters: GDPR is expanding. Privacy-first is becoming everyone's constraint.
- Call to action: "The designers who thrive in the next decade won't be the ones with the best dashboards — they'll be the ones who can validate without them."

**Format:** Long-form article (1,500-2,000 words), published on portfolio blog or LinkedIn/Medium  
**Immediate value:** Positions Tobias as a thought leader on a genuinely differentiated topic. Any hiring manager at a privacy-conscious company (fintech, healthtech, govtech) will find this compelling.

### Quick Win 2: "The Design Leader's Guide to AI User Agency"
**Estimated time:** 3-5 hours  
**Why it's quick:** Direct extension of existing Ninox AI work and ChurchDesk AI alt-text. Tobias has the philosophy ("AI magical when right, annoying when wrong") and the implementation experience.
- Hook: "Every AI feature has a moment where it's wrong. What happens then determines whether users trust it or abandon it."
- Framework: The Agency Spectrum — from "AI decides" to "AI suggests" to "AI explains"
- Case 1: ChurchDesk alt-text — AI does the work, but user can override
- Case 2: Ninox onboarding — AI guides the user, user stays in control
- The principle: Users need to understand *why* AI made a recommendation, and they need an easy escape hatch
- The design patterns: Progressive disclosure of AI reasoning, undo/override affordances, transparent confidence indicators

**Format:** Medium-length article (1,000-1,500 words)  
**Immediate value:** Directly relevant to any company building AI products. Shows Tobias designs AI features, not just uses AI tools. Topical and shareable.

---

### Quick Win 3: "Third Time's the Charm: What I Learned from Two Failed Redesigns"
**Estimated time:** 4-6 hours  
**Why it's quick:** The Spreadshirt story is vivid and personal. It's a retrospective on a completed project with clear lessons.
- Hook: "The seller redesign had failed twice before I arrived. Here's what was different the third time."
- What went wrong before: No user research, stakeholder misalignment, scope creep
- What we did differently: Started with executive workshop, 3-person focused team, research-first approach
- The moment it clicked: The CEO's "60-second journey" vision vs. what users actually wanted
- Lessons learned: Why research wins arguments, how to facilitate executive alignment, when to trust users over stakeholders

**Format:** Narrative article (1,200-1,500 words)  
**Immediate value:** Shows resilience, learning from failure, and executive influence. Story-driven content is memorable and shareable. Demonstrates Tobias can lead complex projects where others have already failed.
