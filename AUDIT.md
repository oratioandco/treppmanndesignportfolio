# Portfolio Case Study Audit

**Date:** 2025-04-25  
**Auditor:** Nora (Career & Projects Agent)  
**Target level:** Head of Design / VP Design at Series C/D, path to M7  
**Baseline:** 43-year-old design leader with 15+ years experience — should read as a seasoned operator, not a mid-weight portfolio piece

---

## 1. ChurchDesk Booking System — "When 'Simple Booking' Isn't Simple"

### Seniority Signal: 🟡 Mid-Weight+

This is the closest to a proper senior case study. The "Design Tension" section — funeral homes need simplicity, priests need autonomy — shows strategic thinking about competing stakeholder needs. The solution architecture and "Design Decisions" sections demonstrate system-level thinking (availability as a platform concept, not just a feature). 

**But:** The framing is still mostly about the *design problem*, not about Tobias *leading* the design. There's no mention of his role, scope, team size, or decision-making authority. Was he the sole designer? Did he lead a team? Did he present to the C-suite? We don't know. The reader has to infer seniority from the quality of the thinking — which is a bet most hiring managers won't make.

### Business Impact: 🔴 Vague

> "Coordination time dropped from days of phone calls to minutes."

This is the only outcome metric, and it's not a metric — it's a vibe. No numbers. No adoption data. No revenue impact. No user count. No before/after comparison. For a Head of Design role, this is the biggest gap. Senior candidates are expected to connect design work to business results. Even approximate numbers ("reduced booking coordination from 3+ phone calls to a single digital request, serving X funeral homes across Y parishes") would be dramatically better.

The line "it became a core platform feature" is promising but unsubstantiated. Which use cases? How many organizations use it? What's the adoption curve?

### Leadership Proof: 🟡 Present but Implicit

The case study shows evidence of:
- Cross-stakeholder negotiation (funeral homes vs. priests vs. parish assistants)
- Strategic product decisions (platform investment vs. feature)
- Systems thinking (unified availability model)

But there's zero explicit leadership language. Who did Tobias manage? Who did he collaborate with? Did he pitch this to stakeholders? Did he mentor anyone through this? The case study reads like a very competent IC designed a complex system — not like a design leader drove organizational change.

### Story Structure: 🟢 Good

This is the best-structured of the three. It has:
- A clear hook (the paradox of simple vs. not-simple)
- Rising tension (opposing stakeholder needs)
- A satisfying resolution (availability model that serves both)
- Visual support throughout

The images help enormously — they break up the text, show process artifacts, and give credibility. The figure captions are well-written and reinforce the narrative.

### Specific Gaps

1. **No role framing** — What was Tobias's title? How big was the team? What was his scope? Add a "My Role" sidebar or section.
2. **No metrics** — This is the #1 gap. Any numbers at all would help: number of parishes, funeral homes served, booking volume, time saved, adoption rate.
3. **No leadership framing** — The case study describes *what was designed*, not *how Tobias led the design*. Needs language about team management, stakeholder alignment, strategic decisions he drove.
4. **No timeline** — How long did this take? What were the phases? Senior leaders ship on timelines.
5. **No reflection** — What would he do differently? What did he learn? This shows growth mindset.

### Quick Wins

- Add a "My Role & Scope" callout box at the top (title, team size, duration, direct reports)
- Replace "days of phone calls to minutes" with actual numbers or a more specific before/after comparison
- Add 2-3 sentences about the team and how Tobias led the work
- Add a "Results" section with 3-4 bullet points, each with a number or specific outcome
- Add a "What I'd Do Differently" paragraph at the end

### Overall Rating: 🟡 Decent

The thinking is senior. The framing is not. This is a solid mid-weight case study that could become a strong senior one with ~2 hours of revision focused on role framing, metrics, and leadership language.

## 2. Ninox — "Redesigning How We Work Together"

### Seniority Signal: 🟢 Senior Leader

This is the only case study that reads like a *leader* wrote it. The reframe from "fix the person" to "fix the system" is exactly the kind of strategic thinking you'd expect from a Head of Design or VP. The section on resistance ("Why should we change for one person?") and how Tobias handled it shows real organizational leadership — not just design craft.

The equity vs. equality framing, the business case for cognitive diversity ("we were losing good ideas"), and the systemic change outcome ("the team learned a new reflex") all signal senior leadership. This is the closest thing in the portfolio to a VP-level case study.

### Business Impact: 🔴 Absent

There are zero business metrics. No team productivity numbers. No retention data. No shipping velocity improvement. No quality metrics. The outcomes are entirely qualitative: "the dynamic shifted," "tension dissolved," "became one of our strongest contributors."

For this case study, that's actually somewhat understandable — it's a people story, not a product story. But even one concrete data point would help: "Team velocity increased X%" or "He went from 0 merged PRs in Q1 to Y in Q3" or " attrition risk reduced."

### Leadership Proof: 🟢 Strong

This is the portfolio's strongest leadership evidence:
- Identified a systemic problem that others saw as a personnel problem
- Made a strategic reframe (diagnose the system, not the person)
- Changed team processes (pre-meeting 1:1s, visual agendas, turn-taking, written decisions)
- Handled resistance with a combination of values and pragmatism
- Created lasting cultural change ("the team learned a new reflex")
- Connected leadership philosophy to design philosophy

This is genuinely good. It shows Tobias operating at the level he's targeting.

### Story Structure: 🟢 Very Good

Excellent narrative arc:
- Hook: "When a team labels someone 'difficult'..." — immediately compelling
- Rising tension: The pattern recognition, the reframe
- Conflict: Team resistance
- Resolution: Systemic change, culture shift
- Coda: Connection to broader leadership philosophy

This is the best-written case study in the portfolio. The prose is clean, the pacing is right, and the emotional beats land. It reads like a real story, not a portfolio template.

### Specific Gaps

1. **No role context** — Same as ChurchDesk. What was Tobias's title at Ninox? How big was the team? How long was this process?
2. **No business metrics** — Even one number would ground this. Team size? Time to resolution? Any downstream impact on shipping?
3. **No visual support** — This is a text wall. Even a simple process diagram, a timeline, or a quote from the engineer (anonymized) would break up the prose and add credibility.
4. **Anonymization is thin** — "One engineer" is fine, but adding something like "I'm sharing this with his permission" or "details adapted for privacy" signals thoughtfulness about consent and ethics.
5. **No connection to Ninox product work** — Tobias did significant product work at Ninox (AI onboarding, alignment system). This case study is only about the people side. Where's the product leadership? It feels like half the story.

### Quick Wins

- Add "My Role & Scope" at the top
- Add one specific outcome metric (even team size or duration counts)
- Add a quote from the engineer or a team member (even anonymized)
- Add a simple timeline or process diagram as a visual break
- Add a paragraph connecting this to Tobias's broader work at Ninox
- Consider splitting: this is a leadership story, not a design case study. It might work better as a "Leadership" section or blog post, with a separate Ninox product case study.

### Overall Rating: 🟡 Decent (borderline 🟢)

The content is strong and the leadership signal is real. But it's missing the structural elements that make a senior portfolio piece complete: role context, metrics, visuals. With those additions, this is a 🟢.

## 3. Modern Practice — "Accelerating Design with AI"

### Seniority Signal: 🔴 Blog Post, Not Case Study

This doesn't read like a case study. It reads like a thought leadership blog post or a conference talk outline. There's no specific project, no specific problem solved, no specific team led. It's a collection of principles and process descriptions.

The Bibel TV section mentions "Head of Product Design" as a title — which is good — but then describes the work in vague terms: "establishing a design system," "cross-platform prototyping," "faster stakeholder alignment." None of this has specifics.

For a Head of Design / VP candidate, this is the weakest signal. It positions Tobias as someone who *thinks about AI design* — not someone who has *shipped AI-powered products with measurable outcomes*.

### Business Impact: 🔴 Absent

Zero metrics. Zero outcomes. Zero business results. The closest thing to an outcome is "tighter feedback loops, more exploration, and better final products" — which is meaningless without evidence.

For a case study about AI (the hottest topic in design leadership right now), this is a massive missed opportunity. AI work should have the most concrete metrics: "reduced prototyping time by X%", "shipped Y features in Z weeks instead of months", "saved $N in contractor costs."

### Leadership Proof: 🟡 Present but Weak

The "Teaching Others" section suggests Tobias leads workshops and trains teams. That's leadership! But it's described in bullet points, not demonstrated through a story. Who did he train? What changed for those teams? What did they ship after?

The Bibel TV section says "Head of Product Design" — but then describes process, not leadership. Managing a design system, prototyping across platforms — these are IC activities unless framed as leading a team to do them.

### Story Structure: 🔴 Flat

There's no narrative. No problem, no tension, no resolution. It's:
1. Here's a shift in design
2. Here's my approach (three principles)
3. Here's where I applied it (vague Bibel TV description)
4. Here's how I teach it (bullet points)
5. Here are more principles

A case study needs a story. This doesn't have one.

### Specific Gaps

1. **No specific project** — What did Tobias actually build? What was the problem? Pick one concrete thing and tell the story.
2. **No metrics** — Especially damaging here because AI work should be measurable. How much faster? How many prototypes? What shipped?
3. **No visual support** — For a case study about AI-powered design, showing the *process* visually would be powerful. Before/after prototypes, AI-generated iterations, the tool chain.
4. **No team context** — Who's on the Bibel TV team? What's the org structure? How does Tobias lead them?
5. **Principles without proof** — "AI amplifies skill" and "speed enables quality" are nice slogans, but without evidence they're just assertions.
6. **Too general** — This could be written by anyone who's used Claude Code for a week. It needs Tobias-specific depth.

### Quick Wins

- **Pick one specific project at Bibel TV** and tell that story. What was the feature? What was the timeline? What did AI enable that wouldn't have been possible otherwise?
- Add before/after numbers for anything: prototype speed, iteration count, stakeholder satisfaction
- Add screenshots or examples of AI-generated prototypes (this is the most visual-friendly topic possible)
- Add a "My Role" section with team size and scope
- If the Bibel TV work is too new to have outcomes, frame it as "In Progress" with interim results and learning

### Overall Rating: 🔴 Weak

This is the portfolio's weakest link. It's not a case study — it's an opinion piece. For an AI product design leader targeting M7 companies, this needs to be rebuilt from scratch around a specific project with specific outcomes.

## Overall Portfolio Assessment

### Strongest Case Study: **Ninox — "Redesigning How We Work Together"**

Despite missing metrics and visuals, this is the only case study that demonstrates real leadership at the level Tobias is targeting. The systemic reframe ("fix the system, not the person"), handling of team resistance, and lasting cultural change all show a senior leader operating at VP-level thinking. With metrics and visuals added, this becomes a genuinely strong piece.

Runner-up: ChurchDesk. The design thinking is senior and the story structure is solid, but it reads as a designer's portfolio piece, not a leader's case study.

### Weakest Case Study: **Modern Practice — "Accelerating Design with AI"**

Not a case study. A blog post. No specific project, no outcomes, no story. For the hottest topic in design leadership, this should be the strongest piece — instead it's the thinnest. Needs a complete rewrite around a concrete AI project.

### Narrative Arc Across All Three

The three case studies cover: **product complexity** (ChurchDesk), **people leadership** (Ninox), and **emerging practice** (Modern Practice/AI). That's actually a solid arc — it shows range across product, people, and process.

**The problem:** The arc is implied, not stated. There's no unifying narrative that ties these together as "who Tobias is." A visitor lands on the portfolio and sees three disconnected case studies. They should see a coherent story:

> "I'm a design leader who builds complex systems (ChurchDesk), transforms teams (Ninox), and stays ahead of the curve (AI practice)."

That story exists in these three pieces, but it's not articulated. The portfolio needs a framing layer — a homepage or about section that positions these three as evidence of a specific kind of leader.

**Missing from the arc entirely:** Tobias's repeated superpower — UX maturity transformation. He's done this at Datameer, Hypoport, Ninox, ChurchDesk. That pattern is his competitive advantage, and none of these case studies name it explicitly.

### Top 3 Things to Fix Across All Case Studies

**1. Add "My Role & Scope" to every case study.**

Title, team size, direct reports, duration, organizational context. This is table stakes for senior portfolios. Without it, every case study could have been done by a freelancer. A Head of Design candidate must show organizational context.

**Template:**
```
My Role: Head of Design / Design Lead  
Team: X designers, Y engineers (cross-functional)  
Duration: X months  
Scope: [Product area, user segment, platform]
```

**2. Add concrete metrics to every case study.**

At minimum, 2-3 specific outcomes per case study. Even approximate numbers are better than vibes. For each:
- ChurchDesk: Number of parishes/organizations, booking volume, time saved, adoption rate
- Ninox: Team size, retention, shipping velocity, or even just timeline to resolution
- Modern Practice: Prototype speed improvement, features shipped, workshop count/attendees

**3. Rebuild Modern Practice as a real case study.**

This is the biggest effort but also the highest ROI. AI experience is Tobias's differentiator in the market — it should be his strongest case study, not his weakest. Pick one concrete project, tell the story, show the outcomes. If Bibel TV work is too fresh, use the ChurchDesk AI alt-text work or the Ninox AI onboarding flow.

---

### Bonus Observations

- **The Ninox case study is actually a leadership essay, not a design case study.** Consider whether the portfolio should have a separate "Leadership" section for this kind of content, freeing up the case study slot for Ninox product work (AI onboarding, alignment system).
- **No "About" framing on the case studies index.** Visitors need context before diving in. A short intro paragraph positioning Tobias as a UX transformation leader would set the frame.
- **The ChurchDesk images are good but the captions are narrative (which is great).** Consider whether the images are actually readable at portfolio scale — some of the file sizes suggest high-resolution complex diagrams that might not scan on mobile.
- **Tone inconsistency:** ChurchDesk is analytical and system-focused. Ninox is warm and narrative. Modern Practice is didactic and principle-heavy. Some tonal range is fine, but they should all feel like the same person.
- **No links to external validation.** No testimonials, no LinkedIn recommendations, no conference talks, no published articles. For a senior candidate, third-party credibility matters.

---

*Audit complete. The raw material is strong — Tobias has done the work. The packaging just doesn't match the seniority level he's targeting. These are fixable gaps, not fundamental weaknesses.*
